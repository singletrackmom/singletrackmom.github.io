Your Title
Designing AI Tools for Student Success: A Research and Development Sabbatical at the Intersection of Technology, Equity, and Community College Student Support
Abstract (150 words max — write for any faculty member, not just educators)
Research at Glendale Community College demonstrates that students who connect with campus support services — advising, counseling, career services, financial aid, and others — persist at significantly higher rates than those who do not. [Insert specific retention figure here — e.g., "students who engaged with at least one support service in their first semester persisted at X% higher rates than those who did not" — pull from your institutional research or ask your dean for the exact stat before submitting] This finding has become a documented institutional priority: GCC faculty are now asked each semester to intentionally connect students to at least one campus service or event, with the retention data to support it. The challenge is access and awareness — the students who most need support are often the least likely to seek it out on their own. Artificial intelligence offers a practical way to close that gap, yet most AI student success tools are being built for large four-year institutions, with little attention to the barriers facing community college students. This sabbatical will investigate how institutions and educational technology companies are currently building AI tools across the full student services spectrum, identify critical gaps for community college populations, and develop one or more prototype tools grounded in GCC's own research on service connection and student persistence. Findings will be shared through a national conference presentation.

If a published report or internal dataset exists, cite it in a footnote.

Goals (what the sabbatical will accomplish)
  1. Investigate the current landscape of AI tools being developed for student success, with particular focus on tools that support students in connecting to and engaging with campus support services — the direct lever identified in GCC's own retention research.
  2. Identify critical gaps in existing AI tools as they apply to community college students, especially first-generation, low-income, and non-traditional learners who face the greatest barriers to proactively seeking support.
  3. Design and develop one or more prototype AI tools informed by GCC's service-connection persistence model, built to reduce the access barriers that prevent students from using the services that most improve their outcomes.
  4. Present findings at a national conference, contributing practical, community-college-centered tools and research to the broader higher education conversation about AI and student success.
💡 Four focused goals is the right scope for one semester. Each should be achievable and verifiable.

Draft Narrative — Personal & Professional Qualifications

Draft text below. Refine language, adjust length, and update the Mesa AI Conference entry once confirmed. Target: ~500–700 words for the final version.

My qualifications for this sabbatical span thirty years of building digital tools, teaching with technology, and advocating for users — a through-line that connects my earliest professional work directly to the AI student success tools I am proposing to develop.

I began my career in 1995 as a graphic designer at United Technologies Automotive, where I designed and developed one of the first Fortune 100 corporate websites — at a time when very few organizations had any web presence at all. From there I moved to Q LTD, an Ann Arbor design firm where I managed client relationships, wrote proposals, oversaw full design processes, and worked across identity, web, and print for clients including the University of Michigan, ProQuest, and SIGGRAPH. This early work was not simply visual design — it was interface development, user communication, and digital product thinking before those terms were in common use.

In 1998 I began teaching college as adjunct faculty, and by 1999 I was directing the Internet Professional program at Washtenaw Community College — one of the first programs of its kind in the country. This was not a graphic design program; it was a full technical curriculum covering HTML, web fundamentals, imaging, audio and video production for the web, and advanced web development, at a time when virtually no institution had formalized this kind of training. I developed the curriculum, managed a 30-computer lab, advised 200 students, and hired and supported part-time faculty. I was teaching in hybrid and flipped classroom formats by 2000, well before those models entered mainstream higher education. That same year I joined ProQuest Information and Learning as Web Marketing Manager, then moved into a Software Product Manager role, where I managed the full development lifecycle for online educational software: defining requirements, conducting usability studies, collaborating with engineering and marketing, and representing products at national EdTech conferences. This is where my formal product management foundation was built — but by that point I had already spent five years building, shipping, and teaching with digital tools.

As Director of Instructional Technology at Glendale Community College from 2004 to 2011, I led LMS operations for a campus with no learning management system when I arrived. I guided the adoption of Blackboard and later contributed to the district RFP process that led to Canvas adoption across the 10-college Maricopa system — a system serving over 250,000 users. I served as a district eLearning Advisory Group co-chair and was selected for the College President's Technology Advisory Taskforce, providing faculty-informed input on technology investment and strategy. I established the campus's first single-point-of-contact instructional technology helpdesk, worked directly with the CIO following a major LMS outage to establish service reliability agreements, and developed templates, standards, and training curriculum used system-wide.

Since 2011 I have served as Residential Faculty in Digital Media Arts at GCC, teaching fully online courses and serving as the departmental Canvas eCourses lead for approximately 30 faculty. I hold an AI Microcredential in Prompt Engineering from Purdue Global, and have served as an OLC/OSCQR Lead Reviewer, Quality Matters Lead Reviewer, and on the eCourses, Open Educational Resources, and PARc Faculty Tenure committees.

Most directly relevant to this sabbatical is my current work integrating AI tools into teaching and course design, and my firsthand experience with GCC's campus-wide student persistence initiative. Faculty at GCC are now asked each semester to make an intentional connection between students and at least one campus support service — advising, counseling, career services, financial aid, basic needs, or others — because our institutional research has documented a clear link between that service engagement and student persistence. I have been participating in this initiative and have seen both its potential and its limitations: the students who most need support are often the hardest to reach through traditional outreach. This direct experience with the problem the sabbatical proposes to address is not incidental — it is the reason I am confident the proposed tools are needed, and the reason I know what a useful tool would actually have to do.

In 2025–2026, I developed an AI literacy resource hub for faculty — a curated, interactive reference tool that helps instructors navigate AI resources organized around higher education priorities. This project deepened my understanding of how faculty engage with AI tools, what barriers they face, and what kinds of scaffolding make adoption sustainable. That experience has sharpened my sense of what is possible with applied AI design — and clarified precisely where the deeper, uninterrupted research and development time that a sabbatical provides would be most impactful. Building student-facing AI tools for advising, early alert, and success coaching requires a scope and level of institutional collaboration that cannot happen alongside a full teaching load. The sabbatical would create the conditions for that work.

I have presented on instructional technology at national and regional conferences including Blackboard World, the League for Innovation STEMTech Conference, the Maricopa Student Success Conference, and multiple Maricopa Technology Conferences. [Update: Mesa AI Conference — add after confirmed.] These experiences have sharpened my ability to communicate complex technology concepts to practitioner audiences and to identify where institutional practice lags behind available tools.

My personal investment in this work is rooted in teaching community college students for over 25 years. I have advised 200+ students, helped at-risk students navigate barriers to completion, and watched firsthand how early intervention and personalized support change outcomes. The tools I am proposing to develop — AI-powered support spanning advising, counseling, career services, financial aid navigation, basic needs triage, disability resources, and veterans services — are not abstract research interests. They are responses to problems I have seen up close, every semester, for my entire career.

I am prepared to conduct this sabbatical with the rigor, independence, and institutional investment it requires. My background spans product management, instructional technology leadership, faculty development, and hands-on AI tool development — an unusual combination that positions me well to research what is being built in the EdTech industry, identify gaps, and contribute meaningful prototypes informed by real community college student needs.
