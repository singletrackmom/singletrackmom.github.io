Personal Research & Concept Development

CopaMigo

Student Connection Tool · PRD v0.1 · April 2026

CopaMigo is a student-designed AI connection tool for Maricopa Community Colleges. The name comes from Copa (Maricopa, and the idea of something that receives and holds you) and Amigo (the peer-to-peer spirit at its core). Students describe their situation in plain language and CopaMigo connects them to the right human support.

01. The Problem

Community college completion rates are low. Nationally, fewer than six in ten community college students return for a second year at the same institution. Part-time students, who make up the majority of community college enrollment, persist to a second fall semester at only 53 percent. These are not students who were not capable of finishing. They are students who started and stopped.

Decades of research point to the same root cause: students who feel connected to their institution and supported by its services persist at significantly higher rates. A 2024 national study of 1,600 students found that those aware of five or more campus support services were 13 percentage points more likely to say they would persist than peers who were aware of fewer. Connection is the mechanism. It shows up in an advising relationship, in knowing how to get a financial aid question answered, in finding a club where you belong, in a peer coach who checks in on you. Students who have a reason to come back come back.

The problem is that the students who most need connection are the least likely to seek it out. They don't know which office handles what, or that a club for people like them exists. They are embarrassed to walk into a building called "Disability Services," or they don't think of themselves as disabled at all. A student who was on an IEP in high school, or who has ADHD or anxiety, often doesn't connect those experiences to the idea that they might qualify for accommodations in college. They just know they're struggling. They won't search for disability services because that word doesn't describe how they see themselves. Every campus has more support than its students can find. The services and communities exist. Students don't find them.

There is also growing evidence that students are already turning to ChatGPT with questions they're afraid to ask a human. A 2024 study found students explicitly value AI because they "can ask any questions they want and don't have to worry about being judged or embarrassed." Kim et al., CHI 2024. A professor at Old Dominion University noted that young people prefer asking AI partly because "they don't have to go to a mental health clinician's office where there may be some stigma associated with coming to the office." ChatGPT has no idea what your campus offers, cannot connect a student to a real advisor, and in serious cases has been documented causing harm. Students need something that receives them the way ChatGPT does, without judgment, any time of day, but routes them toward real answers and real humans.

Many college websites already have a chat widget on the homepage. Students have learned to ignore them. These tools are essentially a search engine with a chat interface; they scan the website for keywords and return links. Type "I'm failing my class and I don't know what to do" into most of them and you'll get a link to the academic calendar. They are not designed to understand a student's situation, and students know it. One of CopaMigo's design challenges is overcoming that earned skepticism. Students will need to learn quickly that this is not one of those bots: built with real thought with real thought, real departmental input, and a genuine intent to connect them to a person who can help.

The persistence case for CopaMigo

A student who finds their way to advising, disability services, the food pantry, or the gaming club is more likely to stay enrolled than one who doesn't. CopaMigo's job is not to answer questions. Its job is to close the gap between students who need connection and students who find it. Every successful routing interaction is a potential persistence intervention.

02. What CopaMigo Does

CopaMigo is a student-facing app that receives a student's situation in plain language (typed or spoken) and routes them to the right campus support with empathy, context, and a warm handoff to a human. It does not replace advisors, counselors, or support staff. It gets students to the door.

01
Student voice, not institutional voice
The interface sounds like a supportive friend, not a college website. No jargon. No "please navigate to the appropriate department."
02
Stigma-aware routing
A student never has to name the office. They describe their situation. CopaMigo figures out who can help and routes accordingly, without labeling the destination until the student is ready.
03
Funnel to humans, not FAQ
The goal is not to answer the question. The goal is to connect the student to a person who can. Every interaction ends with a name, a number, a next step, and when possible a warm handoff.
04
Staff are the knowledge base
Each department submits their top questions. They own and maintain their content. No IT dependency, no vendor content library. The people who know the answers write the answers.
05
Earn trust fast
Students have already learned to dismiss the generic chat widget on their college's homepage. CopaMigo has to prove in the first exchange that it's different: it understood what they said, that the response is real, and that a human is reachable on the other side.

Not every question needs a human

CopaMigo uses a three-tier routing model based on the nature of the question. Some questions have clear, factual answers that don't require a human at all. Others need context before they can be answered and benefit from a short bot interaction first. A few should go directly to a human with no bot layer in between. Getting this distinction right is part of the design.

Tier 1: Bot answers directly Informational and transactional questions with clear, factual answers

These are questions where a correct, current answer exists in the department's knowledge base and no human judgment is required. The bot responds immediately and accurately.

  • When is the next food distribution on campus?
  • What are the tutoring center hours?
  • Where is the food pantry located?
  • What's the last day to withdraw without a W?
  • Is there a gaming club on campus?
  • How do I request a transcript?
  • What documents do I need for FAFSA verification?
  • Where do I go to borrow a laptop?
Tier 2: Bot answers, then offers connection Questions with good general answers, but where a human could help more

These questions have useful bot responses, but the student's specific situation may need a human to go further. The bot gives a helpful first answer and then offers to connect the student with someone.

  • I took ENG101 and MAT120, what should I sign up for next? (Bot can answer based on program requirements; advisor can help with the student's specific situation)
  • What happens to my financial aid if I drop a class? (Bot can explain the general policy; financial aid staff can address the student's specific aid package)
  • How do I request accommodations for my disability? (Bot explains the process; accessibility staff handles the actual intake)
  • I want to transfer to ASU, where do I start? (Bot can provide general steps; advisor can build a personalized plan)
  • Are there any scholarships I can apply for? (Bot can list current opportunities; advisor can help identify eligibility)
Tier 3: Direct to human Sensitive, urgent, or complex situations where a bot response is inadequate or risky

These questions bypass the bot answer layer entirely. The bot's only role is to provide empathy and a warm handoff as quickly as possible. No FAQ, no information dump, no delay.

  • I'm having thoughts of hurting myself (immediate escalation to counseling/988)
  • I don't know how I'm going to eat this week (Basic Needs, with discreet framing)
  • I've been experiencing harassment on campus (Dean of Students, immediately)
  • I'm falling apart and I don't know why (Counseling, with warm referral language)
  • I think I'm going to get evicted (Social work / GCC Cares, urgent)
  • I had a family emergency and I'm going to miss everything (Dean of Students, emergency protocol)

The distinction between Tier 2 and Tier 3 is not just topic: it is urgency and emotional weight. A student asking "how do I get counseling" may be fine with a bot answer. A student saying "I'm falling apart" is not. The system prompt logic should be trained to read the difference.

03. Who Uses CopaMigo

Primary: Students (any stage)

  • Enrolled students who are struggling and don't know where to turn
  • Prospective students who have barriers to enrolling and need a non-scary first contact
  • Returning students who stopped out and are unsure how to come back
  • Dual enrollment students (under 18), with age-appropriate routing and no personal data collected

Secondary: Student Services Staff

  • Content admins: each department submits and maintains their question bank
  • Human handoff recipients: advisors, counselors, coaches who receive routed students
  • Analytics viewers: seeing what students are asking for that isn't currently being met

Tertiary: Institutional Decision-Makers

  • Retention data, service gap analysis, aggregate anonymized usage patterns

07. Human Handoff

Every routed interaction should end with a clear, warm next step toward a human. The quality of the handoff is the product. A phone number on a screen is not a handoff.

Contact card requirements per service

  • Plain-language name of the service (how CopaMigo describes it, not the official title)
  • What this service actually does in one sentence
  • How to contact: walk-in location, phone, email, and/or scheduling link
  • Hours and whether walk-ins are accepted
  • What to say or bring when you show up; this removes the anxiety of not knowing what to do next
  • Whether there is any cost
  • Whether it is confidential (where applicable)
Warm handoff example: "The Peer Success Coach program matches you with a current student who can check in with you weekly and help you navigate campus. You don't need to know what you need; they're trained to figure it out together with you. Contact the Student Success Center to get matched. It's free and it's confidential."

Routing instruction example (system prompt excerpt): "When a student mentions difficulty with deadlines, organization, focus, concentration, anxiety about tests, or any neurodivergent self-description (ADHD, autism, dyslexia, learning disability), route to ACCESSIBILITY_RESOURCES. Do not use the words 'disability' or 'disability services' in your initial response unless the student used those words first. Instead say: 'There are people here who specialize in exactly this kind of support, and you don't need a formal diagnosis to start a conversation with them.'"

No login, no FERPA concerns

CopaMigo does not authenticate students, does not connect to student information systems, and does not access any academic records. There is no login. A student never has to identify themselves to use it. This keeps the tool simple, fast, and free of FERPA complications.

This means CopaMigo works on what students tell it, not on what their record says. A student can say "I've taken ENG101, MAT120, and CIS105, what should I sign up for next in the Computer Science AAS?" and CopaMigo can respond with general guidance based on the program requirements it has been given. What it cannot do is look up that student's actual transcript, check their financial aid status, or pull their degree audit. For anything that requires looking at their actual record, the handoff goes to advising. CopaMigo gets the student to the door; the advisor opens it.

  • No login required, no student data stored
  • No student-identifying information sent to the AI, only the content of the conversation
  • Peer connection opt-in requires explicit consent and is revocable
  • Aggregate analytics are fully anonymized before any staff-facing reporting

11. Build Order

Phase 1: Prototype (2-3 weeks)

  • Single HTML file, voice-to-text input, Claude API routing
  • System prompt with 3-4 hardcoded department modules (advising, financial aid, disability, basic needs)
  • Empathy-first response + stigma-aware routing for the core scenarios
  • Static contact cards, no live data, just test the concept
  • Test with real students from student senate or student government

Phase 2: Pilot (4-6 weeks after prototype)

  • All departments have submitted question banks
  • Dynamic contact cards with live hours and links
  • Peer connection opt-in feature
  • Crisis escalation tested and validated with counseling staff
  • Deploy at one college, track for one semester

Phase 3: Scale

  • Multi-campus deployment with campus-specific department modules
  • Staff analytics dashboard
  • Spanish language support
  • Integration with the institution's existing student portal app (confirm feasibility with IT)
  • Appointment scheduling integration where departments are ready

12. Staffing, Funding, and Deployment

Built by students, for students

CopaMigo should be designed and built by students, not just for them. A student development team brings authenticity to the voice and the routing logic that no staff-built tool can replicate. Students know what other students are actually afraid to ask. A work-study program could fund the development team, with a small grant covering the initial prototype and pilot phases. Grant funding is appropriate here: this is applied research with a direct retention outcome, and community college student success grants are well-established at both the state and federal level.

Peer-to-peer staffing model

The human layer of CopaMigo is peer support staff, not counselors. Approximately 10 students per campus, available during most daytime and evening hours, trained to receive warm handoffs from the AI and connect students to the right service. This is not crisis counseling. It is peer navigation: a student who knows the campus, knows the services, and can say "I've been there, here's who helped me." Work-study is the right funding vehicle for ongoing peer staff once the grant phase ends.

Crisis routing goes outside the peer layer

Tier 3 crisis situations route to professional resources, not students. The campus social worker and social work interns are the appropriate first human contact for situations that rise above peer navigation. When counseling isn't immediately available, and on many community college campuses counselors are primarily referral-based rather than providing ongoing therapy on-site, the escalation path goes to a community crisis line or agency, not to a peer. The system should make this handoff clear, warm, and fast.

Two deployment paths

Option A: Integrated
  • CopaMigo connects into the existing My Maricopa Student Portal app
  • Students already have the app; no new download required
  • Requires OIT coordination and API access to the portal
  • Higher reach, longer timeline to launch
Option B: Standalone
  • A student-designed tool built independently of the district app
  • Students build it, students brand it, students own the voice
  • Faster to prototype and pilot; no institutional dependency
  • Can be adopted by the app later once proven

Option B is the right starting point. Build it standalone, prove it works, then bring it to the district.

13. Competitive Landscape

Several products exist in this space. None combines stigma-aware empathetic intake, staff-trained departmental knowledge, and a design philosophy explicitly built around funneling students to humans rather than answering their questions. Here are the five closest comparisons.

ASU Hey Sunny heysunny.asu.edu ↗

What it is: Arizona State University's student-facing chatbot, launched 2018, now covering admissions, financial aid, deadlines, housing, campus life, and student parent support. Reaches 36,000+ students via SMS and a web interface. In its first year, saved 492 hours of staff time and achieved a 78% student satisfaction rate. Students can text Sunny or chat on the website and get responses drawn from a curated knowledge base, with human staff reviewing and improving answers over time.

Where it falls short for CopaMigo's vision: Sunny is primarily a proactive outreach tool; the institution texts students, not the other way around. It is knowledge base and FAQ-driven, not empathetic triage. It does not assess a student's situation and route them to the right human support. It also lives at a major research university with a large technical team; there is no accessible model for community colleges to replicate it. But Sunny proves that students will engage with a conversational campus bot, and that the voice should be warm and relatable, not institutional.

Mainstay (now part of Lemnis) mainstay.com ↗

What it is: A behaviorally-intelligent student engagement platform used at 200+ colleges, recently acquired by the nonprofit Lemnis alongside InsideTrack (student coaching). Mainstay focuses on proactive SMS outreach grounded in behavioral science; the institution reaches out to students at key moments with empathetic, personalized messages designed to reduce summer melt, improve FAFSA completion, and keep students on track. Georgia State's Pounce chatbot (22% reduction in summer melt, 50% drop in withdrawal due to outstanding balances) was built on Mainstay.

Where it falls short for CopaMigo's vision: Mainstay is outbound (it contacts students). CopaMigo is inbound (it receives students who reach out when they're struggling. Mainstay is also not stigma-aware in routing; it handles the transactions well but doesn't specifically address the student who doesn't know what they need or is embarrassed to ask.

Ivy & Ocelot (by Gravyty) gravyty.com/ivy-ocelot ↗

What it is: The merger of Ocelot and Ivy.ai, now owned by Gravyty, serving 500+ institutions and 8 million+ students. Answers 97% of student questions without human escalation, integrates with SIS and CRM systems, supports two-way texting and live chat escalation, and has a multilingual content library covering financial aid, admissions, advising, and registrar questions. The most widely deployed higher ed chatbot platform in the U.S.

Where it falls short for CopaMigo's vision: Ocelot excels at transactional FAQ: "when is the FAFSA deadline," "what documents do I need," "how do I add a class." It is not designed for the student who types "I have ADHD and I can't function." The routing is topic-based, not situation-based, and the voice is institutional rather than student-centered. It is also primarily a website widget, not a standalone app with a student voice.

EdSights edsights.io ↗

What it is: An AI-powered student success platform focused on early alert and proactive check-ins, particularly for community colleges. EdSights sends automated text check-ins to students, collects data on how they're doing, and flags at-risk students for advisor follow-up. It is specifically designed for the community college context and student population.

Where it falls short for CopaMigo's vision: EdSights is analytics and early-alert driven; it identifies students who may be struggling based on survey responses and flags them for staff. The student is not the initiator. There is no empathetic intake layer for a student to describe their situation in their own words and receive a warm routing response in return.

LearnWise AI learnwise.ai ↗

What it is: A newer comprehensive AI support platform that goes beyond chatbot to include LMS integration, proactive nudges, bilingual support, and real-time analytics. LearnWise can ingest a student's actual academic data to provide contextually relevant responses and identifies content gaps in the knowledge base automatically. Claims to be the most comprehensive end-to-end student AI assistant platform currently available.

Where it falls short for CopaMigo's vision: LearnWise is a full institutional platform requiring significant implementation, not a lightweight, community-college-accessible tool. Its strength is academic data integration, but CopaMigo's core differentiation is in the empathetic, stigma-aware routing layer and the staff-trains-their-own-piece model. LearnWise is also expensive and primarily oriented toward four-year institutions.

The gap: All five products are institution-facing at their core; they route messages from the institution to students, or answer FAQ from a knowledge base. None is specifically designed around the student who doesn't know what they need, is embarrassed to ask, or would never walk into a building called "Disability Services." That is the gap CopaMigo is designed to fill.
