From Bookmarks to Infrastructure
A Faculty Practitioner's Account of Building AI-Powered Learning Environments at a Community College

Michelle Blomberg · Glendale Community College, Digital Media Arts · Started March 2026 · Living Document

Abstract

This paper documents the development of two interconnected AI-powered tools built by a faculty member at a community college with no institutional support, no dedicated budget, and no prior software development background: Cultivate, a personal learning environment for faculty professional development, and Render, an AI-assisted career readiness platform for students in a Digital Media Arts capstone course. Both tools are grounded in connectivist learning theory and grew directly from problems the author was trying to solve in her own practice. This paper traces the origin story of each tool, the design decisions made along the way, the role of AI as a co-developer, and the unresolved questions about sustainability, equity, and institutional adoption that remain. It is written for faculty practitioners, instructional designers, and EdTech researchers interested in what grassroots AI tool development looks like from the inside.

1. The Problem This Started With

In 2024, like many faculty members, I found myself trying to learn about artificial intelligence while simultaneously teaching, advising, running a publication, serving on committees, and managing everything else that comes with a full-time faculty position at a community college. The problem was not a lack of resources. It was too many resources in too many places with no coherent way to manage them.

I had bookmarks scattered across three browsers. Coursera courses I had enrolled in and abandoned. YouTube playlists I could not find again. Email newsletters I was not reading. Organization subscriptions through Maricopa — EDUCAUSE, the Online Learning Consortium, the National AI Advisory Committee, and others — that I was not using because I did not know where to start. What I needed was not more content. I needed a way to tend what I already had.

Around the same time, I was experiencing something a lot of people experienced in that period: a low-grade anxiety fed by social media and news consumption. The easiest thing to reach for on my phone was a feed of content that left me feeling worse, not better. I recognized I needed to replace that habit with something more intentional. These two problems turned out to have the same solution.

What I needed was not more content. I needed a way to tend what I already had.

I also had a theoretical framework sitting in the back of my mind from my 2017 MEd research at Northern Arizona University. My graduate work focused on connectivism and personal learning environments — the idea that learning happens through the connections we build and maintain, and that a learner's environment should be something they construct and tend rather than something delivered to them. I had written about this as an abstract principle. Building Cultivate was the first time I applied it to my own life.

2. Cultivate: Building the Faculty Tool

Origin and First Version

Cultivate began in late 2024 as a simple HTML page where I collected links. It quickly became something more structured: a place to manage my professional development plan, track conference proposals, organize sabbatical thinking, and curate the news and resources I actually wanted to read. I built it in HTML, CSS, and vanilla JavaScript because those were the only tools I had access to on my iPad while traveling, and because I wanted something that would work offline.

The offline requirement was not an afterthought. I travel frequently, I work in environments with unreliable connectivity, and I had grown tired of tools that required a constant internet connection to function. Building for offline-first meant accepting certain constraints — no database, no user accounts, data stored in the browser via localStorage — but those constraints turned out to shape the tool in ways that made it more private, more portable, and more honest about what it actually was: a personal tool, not a platform.

The RSS Feed and the Doom-Scrolling Problem

One of the most consequential design decisions was building a live RSS news feed directly into Cultivate. The feed draws from 62 sources across five subject areas: AI in education, EdTech, student success, UX and design, and product management. Sources include EDUCAUSE, the Online Learning Consortium, Stanford HAI, EdSurge, MIT Technology Review, and organizations Maricopa already subscribes to that many faculty never use.

The purpose of the feed was not just professional development. It was to give me something intentional to read instead of social media. When the urge to reach for my phone arrives, I now open Cultivate instead. The feed is curated, relevant to my actual work, and does not have an engagement algorithm designed to make me feel bad. This behavioral design insight — that a tool can replace a harmful habit by meeting the same underlying need in a healthier way — became an important part of how I thought about Render later.

Claude was used throughout development as a co-developer: suggesting sources, writing and debugging code, helping think through information architecture, and serving as a sounding board when design decisions were unclear. This is worth naming because it is part of the story. These tools were not built by a software engineer. They were built by a faculty member with a design background, a theoretical framework, a problem to solve, and access to an AI that could write code.

Structure and Current State

Cultivate currently consists of six interconnected HTML pages: an AI Resources Hub, a Professional Development Plan, a Mesa Summit conference proposal page, a PD Log, a UX Toolkit, and a CV page. The RSS architecture uses a 3-proxy waterfall with a 24-hour localStorage cache, so articles load even when primary sources are unavailable. A full Product Requirements Document defines user stories, data model, success metrics, and adaptation pathways for other institutions.

The tool is hosted at singletrackmom.github.io and is used daily. It is the content backbone of the AI Advisory Committee proposal being developed at GCC and serves as the architectural model for Render.

3. Render: Translating the Model for Students

The Insight That Started It

The connection between Cultivate and Render was not immediately obvious. It came from noticing that everything students do in AVC 248, the Design Self Promotion capstone course I teach at Glendale Community College, maps to the same kind of infrastructure. Students in that course spend a semester doing career readiness work: writing resumes, researching jobs, practicing interviews, identifying skills gaps, building a networking plan. When the semester ends and their Canvas access expires, that work disappears. They leave with finished assignments. They do not leave with a system they can keep using.

That gap — between what students build inside a course and what they carry with them after graduation — is the problem Render was designed to solve. A personal learning environment for students in the same spirit as Cultivate: something they construct, tend, and own.

Design Decisions and Trade-offs

Render is built as a single HTML file with vanilla JavaScript and no framework dependencies. This was a deliberate choice rooted in the same offline-first, low-barrier principles as Cultivate. A single file that can be saved to a computer and used without an internet connection is more equitable than a web application that requires a subscription, an account, and a reliable connection. For community college students navigating financial pressure, device limitations, and irregular schedules, that matters.

Authentication in the current prototype uses a first-name-only login. This was chosen to protect student privacy: no student ID, no email address, no institutional credentials stored anywhere. Data lives in the browser's localStorage, keyed to the student's first name. This approach was intentional but is also one of the most significant unresolved questions in the project. localStorage is device-specific and not durable. A student who switches devices or clears their browser loses their data. Solving this at scale requires a backend, which requires a server, which requires a budget.

The Career Services Connection

One design feature of Render that goes beyond individual student benefit is the Career Services data pipeline. When a student saves a job listing in the Job and Client Log, an anonymized summary — job title, company name, and a brief AI-generated description — is sent to GCC Career Services via a Google Apps Script endpoint. The student's name and personal details are never included.

The purpose of this feature is institutional. Career Services cannot build employer relationships they do not know students want. Faculty advisors cannot advocate for internship opportunities they do not know exist. By surfacing employer interest in an anonymized, aggregated form, Render gives the institution data it currently does not have. This connects directly to research on student persistence: students who feel connected to campus support services succeed at higher rates. Building that connection into the tool's data architecture, rather than treating it as an add-on, was a deliberate choice.

The AI Layer

Render uses Claude (Anthropic) as the AI engine across 15 distinct functions. These include skills gap analysis from job descriptions, tailored cover letter generation, LinkedIn outreach message drafting, role-specific interview question generation with coaching feedback, and a personalized 90-day post-graduation plan with a concrete weekly schedule. Each function is structured so that AI responds to what the student has already built, not to a blank prompt. A student's goals, saved jobs, and skills profile are passed as context to every AI call. This is what makes the tool different from asking students to use ChatGPT on their own: the interface does the prompt engineering so the student does not have to.

4. Theoretical Grounding

Both tools are rooted in connectivism, the learning theory developed by George Siemens and Stephen Downes in the early 2000s, which argues that knowledge exists in networks and that learning is the act of forming and maintaining connections within those networks. Connectivism was developed in response to networked digital environments, but it describes something that predates the internet: the idea that a learner's environment should be something they build and tend, not something that is delivered to them.

The practical realization of connectivism is the personal learning environment, or PLE. A PLE is not a platform or a product. It is a practice: the ongoing act of curating, connecting, and reflecting on resources in ways that support your own learning goals. Cultivate and Render are both PLEs in this sense. Cultivate gives faculty a place to tend their professional learning infrastructure. Render gives students a place to tend their career development infrastructure. Neither delivers content to a passive user. Both require the user to put something in to get something out.

The irony of building connectivist tools with AI is not lost on me. The whole premise of connectivism is that the learner constructs their own network. AI could shortcut that construction in ways that undermine it. The design response in both tools is to use AI to respond to what the user has already built, not to replace the building. AI in Render does not write a student's resume from scratch. It suggests edits to a draft the student wrote. It does not generate a networking plan out of nothing. It drafts an outreach message from a contact the student logged. The distinction matters.

5. Unresolved Questions

API Cost and Sustainability Who pays for the Claude API calls in a classroom pilot? Currently funded through a personal account. Not sustainable at scale. Potential paths: Anthropic education program, Perkins grant funding, GCC Foundation, departmental budget, or OIT partnership. Estimated cost for 24 students over one semester: $50 to $150, depending on engagement. Small enough to fund through internal grants; large enough to require a formal budget line at scale.
Authentication and Data Durability LocalStorage is device-specific and not durable. Students who switch devices or clear their browser lose their data. Long-term solution requires a backend with proper authentication. Current workaround: JSON export and import. This is a known limitation of the prototype and a primary focus of post-pilot development.
Student Privacy and AI What data is sent to Claude, and what are students' rights regarding that data? Current approach: only the content the student has entered into the tool is sent as context. No biometric data, no institutional ID, no contact information. A plain-language disclosure is planned for the login screen. FERPA implications of the Career Services data pipeline need formal review before full deployment.
Institutional Adoption Render was built on personal equipment, with a personal API key, outside of any institutional process. This is how faculty innovation actually happens at most community colleges. The question is how to move from a personal project to an institutionally supported tool without losing the agility that made it possible to build in the first place. OIT interest exists but budget does not. Grant funding is the most likely bridge.
Scalability to Other CTE Programs The career readiness framework in Render is specific to Digital Media Arts but the architecture is not. Nursing, culinary arts, automotive technology, and other CTE programs at GCC and across the Maricopa district have analogous capstone courses with analogous career launch needs. District-wide adaptation is a post-pilot goal, pending pilot results.

6. What This Means for Other Faculty

The tools described in this paper were not built by a software engineer or an instructional technologist with dedicated time and institutional support. They were built by a faculty member with a full teaching load, a design background, a graduate theory framework, and access to an AI that could write code. That is worth saying plainly because it changes what the story is about.

This is not a story about what institutions can build when they invest in AI infrastructure. It is a story about what faculty can build when they have a clear problem, a theoretical framework, and a tool that meets them where they are. The implication for other faculty is not "go build this." It is: the barrier to building tools that serve your students is lower than it has ever been, and the most useful tools you can build are the ones that solve problems you already understand deeply because you live inside them every day.

The implication for institutions is different. Faculty are building these tools whether institutions support them or not. The question is whether those institutions want to be part of what happens next.
