What you will study

Four Movements, One Turbulent Decade

Movement I of IV

Arts & Crafts

c. 1880 – 1920 · England & America

A rebellion against industrial production. William Morris and the Kelmscott Press believed beauty required handcraft, natural motifs, and honest materials. The machine was the enemy; the medieval workshop was the ideal.

Movement II of IV

Vienna Secession

c. 1897 – 1920 · Austria

Young Viennese artists broke from the conservative Academy to pursue a total art — Gesamtkunstwerk — where painting, furniture, typography, and architecture formed one unified whole. Gold, geometry, and the grid.

Movement III of IV

Italian Futurism

c. 1909 – 1920 · Italy

Marinetti's manifesto declared war on the past. Futurists worshipped speed, machines, and violence. Their parole in libertà — words in freedom — shattered the orderly printed page and invented a new typographic energy.

Movement IV of IV

Russian Constructivism

c. 1915 – 1932 · Russia

After the 1917 Revolution, designers asked: what should art look like in a communist state? El Lissitzky and Rodchenko answered with pure geometry, red and black, and the conviction that design must serve the people.
